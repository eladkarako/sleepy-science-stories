The station has a smell that every astronaut notices.

This is one of those facts about the station that you do not find in the
technical manuals. You find it in the memoirs and interviews of the people who
have been there. The International Space Station has a smell.

Astronauts returning from a spacew walk who have been outside in the vacuum of
space in their suits describe noticing it when they remove their helmets back
inside the airlock. A sharp, slightly metallic electric smell that some compare
to welding or burning metal, others to seared steak, others to something more
like gunpowder. It clings to their suits and gloves.

It fades after a few minutes inside the station as the scrubbed and regulated

Space itself, of course, is a vacuum and has no smell in the traditional sense.

What the astronauts are picking up is thought to be the result of atomic oxygen
in the very upper reaches of the atmosphere interacting with the materials of
their suits or possibly volatile compounds released from reactive particles in
the environment clinging to the exterior surfaces of their equipment.

Inside the station itself, away from the airlock, the air has its own character.

Astronauts describe it as clean but slightly stale with undertones of machinery
and electronics. There are sometimes stronger smells when food is being

A reminder of kitchens and home.

For something that sounds as simple as rest, sleeping on the International
Space Station requires some careful thought and a bit of getting used to.

In microgravity, there is no comfortable surface to lie down on.

There is no mattress, no pillow, no bed frame. You float.

The most notable is that you might drift.

The station has been used to study how fire behaves in microgravity.

Fire on a space station is one of the most serious emergencies imaginable.

In a sealed pressurized environment with nowhere to go and no firefighting
aircraft to call, a fire is an immediate threat to life.

The station has fire detection and suppression systems and the crew trains
extensively for fire emergencies.

But beyond the safety systems, fire itself is also a subject of scientific
interest in space. On Earth, fire burns with a familiar teardrop shape.

The hot gases produced by combustion rise through convection,
drawing in fresh oxygen from below and creating that distinctive upward
pointing flame. In microgravity, there is no convection.

Hot gases do not rise because there is no up.

A flame in microgravity forms a sphere, a round ball of blue and sometimes
faintly yellowish fire, spreading outward in all directions equally.

Experiments conducted in the station's combustion research rack have studied
how various materials burn in these conditions.

The research has practical applications for designing fire suppression systems
for future spacecraft. It has also revealed some genuinely unexpected phenomena.

Researchers found that flames they believed had gone out were in some cases
continuing to burn in a cold, dim, nearly invisible mode called cool flames.

These cool flames burn at temperatures and through chemical reactions that are
different from normal combustion and they produce different byproducts.

This was not something researchers had predicted.

It was a discovery made possible by the unique environment of space where the
absence of gravity removed variables that had always been present in combustion
research on Earth and revealed something that had been hidden underneath.

The station has a way of doing that, of stripping away the assumptions we carry
from growing up on the ground and showing us what is underneath them.

Fire behaves differently aboard the station than anywhere on Earth.

On Earth, fire has a familiar shape.

A candle flame stretches upward into a teardrop point because the hot gases it
produces are lighter than the cooler air around them.

And gravity pulls the cooler air downward and inward, feeding fresh oxygen to
the base of the flame and pushing the hot gases up into that characteristic
elongated form. The shape of every campfire, every gas burner,
every struck match you have ever seen is the shape that gravity gives to
burning. On the station in microgravity, there is no gravitational convection.

Hot gases do not rise because there is no up for them to rise toward.

Instead, a flame in microgravity burns as a small, nearly perfect sphere,
glowing faintly with a cooler, bluer light than its Earth counterpart.

It consumes the oxygen immediately around it and produces carbon dioxide and
water vapor in a tiny shell around itself.

And without the convective flow that on Earth would carry fresh air to it,
it tends to burn at lower temperatures and with less intensity.

This changes almost everything about fire safety aboard the station.

The crew trains extensively for fire scenarios and the station carries fire
suppression equipment. But the behavior of fire in microgravity is different
enough from fire on Earth that the training is specialized.

A smoldering fire in microgravity can be difficult to detect at first because
it produces less visible flame.

Smoke particles without gravity to cause them to rise can disperse in
unexpected directions. The station's air scrubbing systems help detect
combustion products early, and the crew carries out regular drills to ensure
they know how to respond. Fire is one of the scenarios aboard the station that
is taken most seriously. In a sealed environment with no immediate possibility
of evacuation, a fire is not an emergency. you can simply escape from.

The response must be fast, disciplined, and effective.

And the engineers and trainers who prepared for this scenario had to think
carefully about how to prepare crews for a threat that behaves by different
rules than anything they had encountered before.

The station is slowly losing altitude and requires regular boosts to maintain
its orbit. The station orbits in what is technically referred to as low Earth
orbit. But even at 400 km, the atmosphere is not completely gone.

The extreme upper reaches of the atmosphere exist even at that altitude.

a region so thin that it is essentially a vacuum, but not quite a perfect one.

The molecules up there are so sparse that the distance between them is enormous,
but they are present. And as the station moves through them at 7.

7 kilometers/s, they create a tiny but real drag force.

Over weeks and months, this atmospheric drag gradually lowers the station's
orbit. If nothing were done about it, the station would eventually decay into
the denser, lower atmosphere and burn up, just as uncontrolled spacecraft
eventually do. To prevent this, the station's orbit must be regularly boosted
back up. Thruster firings either from the station's own thrusters or from
visiting spacecraft that are docked and can fire their engines to push the
station higher are performed periodically to maintain the desired altitude.

These boosts are part of the quiet constant maintenance of the station.

They happen without fanfare.

Sometimes during the night, during the crew's sleep time, invisible to anyone
not watching the orbital mechanics data.

A brief firing of thrusters, a slight nudge upward, and then the station
continues on its way at the altitude it has always been.

The atmospheric drag that tried to pull it down temporarily reversed.

The eventual end of the station will come in a controlled descent.

When the nation's operating it decide that it has reached the end of its useful
life, a series of deliberate firings will lower its orbit gradually until it
enters the upper atmosphere and the friction begins.

The station's position relative to the sun changes as it orbits,

Photography from the International Space Station is some of the most
extraordinary photography ever taken by human beings.

The light in low Earth orbit does not behave the way light behaves anywhere on
the surface of the Earth. There is no atmosphere to diffuse it.

Sunlight in space is raw and direct and blindingly intense,

The station serves as a relay point for communications, research,

The station experiences both extreme cold and extreme heat within minutes of
each other. The thermal environment of the International Space Station is one
of the most challenging aspects of operating there and the engineering
solutions that keep the crew comfortable inside represent some of the most
sophisticated temperature management systems ever built.

When the station is in full sunlight, solar radiation heats exposed surfaces to
temperatures that can exceed 120° C.

When it passes into the shadow of the Earth, those same surfaces begin to cool
rapidly. And in the full shadow, temperatures on uninsulated surfaces can drop
to nearly -50° C. This swing of more than 250° happens multiple times in every
orbit over and over, day after day, year after year.

The materials used in the station's construction must survive this repeated
thermal cycling without cracking or degrading.

The station is covered in multi-layer insulating blankets.

Layer upon layer of thin reflective materials separated by vacuum gaps that
prevent heat transfer. The outer surfaces are coated with special materials
that balance the absorption of solar radiation with the emission of heat,
keeping temperatures within manageable ranges.

Inside the thermal control system uses a network of pipes carrying ammonia as a
coolant. Waste heat from the electronics, the life support systems,
the cruise bodies, and everything else that generates heat inside the station
is collected by this coolant loop circulated to large external radiators and
radiated away into space. The radiators are thin rectangular panels attached to
the station's truss structure, and they glow faintly in the infrared as they
shed the station's waste heat into the void.

The crew inside experiences none of this directly.

The temperature in the habitation modules is kept comfortable,
roughly 22° C year round. The drama of the external thermal environment is
entirely invisible from where the astronauts live and work.

They move through a space that feels like a well-managed office building,
while all around them, just centimeters outside the hull, the temperature
swings wildly enough to destroy unprotected metal. That is in its own quiet way.

One of the most remarkable things about the station, the contrast between what
the crew experiences inside and what the laws of physics demand outside,
bridged by systems so welld designigned that the transition is invisible.

Engineering that makes the impossible feel ordinary.

and science of the station itself.

It is the study of what happens to a human being who stays in space not for

Knowledge of what happens so that the next generation of spacecraft and

one talks about. There are some aspects of life on the International Space

On Earth, a toilet works through gravity.

Waste falls, water washes it away, and the whole process relies entirely on the
fact that things on Earth tend to move downward.

In microgravity, none of this works. Waste does not fall. Water does not flow.

Without some alternative mechanism, what happens next would be not only
unpleasant but genuinely dangerous to the health of the crew and the
cleanliness of the station's air supply.

The solution is a toilet that works through airflow rather than water flow.

A powerful suction system draws air past the user in a controlled direction,
carrying waste away and into collection containers in much the way a vacuum
cleaner operates, though with rather more precision required.

Solid waste is collected into bags that are then sealed and eventually disposed

incinerating their contents.

Liquid waste, as mentioned in the discussion of water recycling,
is processed and eventually returned to the station's water supply.

spaceflight life that requires the most specific instruction and the most
careful practice before launch.

There are training devices on the ground that familiarize crews with the
geometry and the procedure. The tolerance for error is minimal.

The consequences of inaccuracy in a sealed spacecraft are not abstract.

station's operation, and the latest versions are considerably more effective
and easier to use than the original equipment.

their anatomy. The redesigned system addressed this more thoughtfully.
