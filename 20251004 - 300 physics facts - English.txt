Pascal's law is a beautifully simple rule that underpins everything from car
brakes to hydraulic lifts.

It states that a change in pressure applied to a confined fluid is transmitted
equally and unddeinished in all directions.

Push on the fluid at one spot and every part of the container feels it.

This is why a small force applied on a narrow piston can lift a car if that
piston is connected through fluid to a larger piston.

The pressure is the same on both, but the larger area multiplies the force.

In words, force divided by area is equal to pressure.

So equal pressure across unequal areas creates unequal forces.

It is a law that takes the invisible jostling of molecules and turns it into
everyday power tools. The hydrostatic paradox is another gem of fluid thinking.

Imagine three containers of different shapes, but all filled to the same height

another taper inward and the third stand straight like a cylinder.

The pressure at the base is identical for all three.

It depends only on depth, density, and gravity.

Expressed as pressure equals density multiplied by G multiplied by height.

The total force on the base is simply this pressure time the base area.

It does not matter if the container flares outward or narrows above.

The weight of the liquid distributed along the sides balances the forces so
that the bottom only feels the height.

This paradox was striking to early scientists who assumed shape should matter.

Fluids revealed that height rules all in moving fluids.

Conservation of mass appears as the continuity equation.

When a fluid meets a blunt object, another phenomenon emerges, flow separation.

As the fluid wraps around the body, at some point it can no longer follow the

This low pressure zone drags back on the body, creating pressure drag far
greater than skin friction alone.

A flatbacked shape leaves a gaping vacuum region that resists forward motion.

increases until it matches the free flowing value.

This thin region of changing speed is called the boundary layer.

At low speeds or over short distances, the boundary layer remains lamina.

Smooth orderly sheets of flow slide past one another like pages of a book.

collapses into turbulence.

The transition point depends on a dimensionless number called Reynolds number
which compares inertial to viscous forces.

Below a certain threshold, lamina flow is stable. Above it, turbulence reigns.

This shift matters greatly because turbulent boundary layers mix more,
carry more momentum and alter drag characteristics.

The skin friction drag on a flat plate behaves very differently depending on
whether the boundary layer is lamina or turbulent.

In a turbulent regime, the friction coefficient scales roughly with the inverse
square root of Reynolds number.

In words, as Reynolds number grows, skin friction decreases, but only slowly.

This means that at very high speeds, skin friction does not vanish.

It remains significant, but it grows more gently than one might expect.

Engineers studying aircraft wings, ship hulls, or pipelines must take this
scaling into account because at high Reynolds numbers, the frictional losses
across enormous surfaces can become a major part of the total resistance.

When it comes to reducing drag, the shape of the body matters more than the
texture of the surface. A perfectly smooth but blunt body creates a large wake
behind it, generating pressure drag that dwarfs skin friction,
streamlining a body, tapering its rear so the flow reattaches smoothly,
shrinks this wake dramatically.

That is why airplanes have sleek fuselages and why fish evolve tapered bodies.

Streamlining sacrifices a little surface area, which might increase skin
friction slightly, but the payoff is a massive reduction in pressure drag.

In practice, this is the key to efficient movement through fluids,
not polishing endlessly, but shaping wisely.

Circulation here means the net rotation of flow around the wing,

The theorem captures the essence of lift without resorting to popular

It simply says that if circulation exists, lift exists.

Statistical physics takes the unruly world of countless particles and tames it

Phase transitions are where matter reveals some of its most dramatic tricks,
